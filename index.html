Where Is the M1 Receptor Brake on OPC Differentiation Strongest? A Brain-Region Map

Cathy Liu · cathyliu014@gmail.com · personal exploratory project, July 2026
remyelination muscarinic M1 receptor OPC differentiation clemastine multiple sclerosis Allen Brain Atlas Python

The Green lab has pioneered the clinical translation of remyelinating therapies for multiple sclerosis, demonstrating that clemastine fumarate — originally an antihistamine — promotes myelin repair through oligodendrocyte differentiation. The mechanistic basis was established by Poon et al. (PNAS 2024): the muscarinic M1 receptor acts as a differentiation brake on OPCs, and selective M1 antagonism releases this brake, allowing progenitors to mature into myelinating oligodendrocytes. This finding also explains why clemastine works — it has significant M1 antagonist activity alongside its antihistamine effects.

A question I found myself thinking about after reading this work: where in the brain is this M1 brake most active? If M1 receptor expression in OPC-rich regions is high, those regions have more therapeutic leverage from M1 antagonism — but may also be doing less spontaneous remyelination, because their progenitors are under stronger tonic inhibition. Conversely, regions with high OL lineage density but low Chrm1 expression may be remyelinating more efficiently already.

I mapped ISH expression of muscarinic receptor subtypes, OPC/OL lineage markers, and mature myelin genes across eight brain regions using the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas, and built a simple "therapeutic opportunity map" to visualize the relationship.

What this is. Personal exploratory analysis, not novel research. Data from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas (Allen Institute for Brain Science, 2004), via the public RMA API. ISH expression energy is a bulk regional average and reflects the full mix of cell types in each region — it cannot isolate OPC-specific Chrm1 expression (which Poon et al. characterized at the protein level in human MS tissue). The analysis is intended as a framework for thinking about regional differences, not as a cell-type-resolved map.

Muscarinic subtypes and OL lineage markers across brain regions

Chrm1 (M1) peaks in isocortex (16.4) and hippocampus (15.9) — the same regions where Olig2 (OL lineage density) is also highest (17.1 and 14.5). The co-localization of M1 expression and OL lineage cells in cortex and hippocampus is consistent with Poon et al.'s finding that M1 is expressed in oligodendroglial cells specifically. These two regions are also where cortical MS lesions and hippocampal atrophy are most clinically significant for cognitive outcomes — suggesting M1 antagonism may be most relevant precisely where cognitive morbidity is highest.
Chrm4 (M4) peaks sharply in striatum (12.5) while Chrm1 is moderate there (13.1). The striatum has a different muscarinic subtype balance than cortex — M4 is the dominant subtype in medium spiny neurons. This means that in striatal MS lesions, M1-selective antagonism (as in Poon et al. 2024) would be less confounded by off-target M4 effects compared to earlier non-selective approaches. Subtype selectivity matters most in regions with mixed M1/M4 expression.
Mature myelin genes (Mbp 11.9, Plp1 21.7, Cnp 10.5) all peak in midbrain, where Chrm1 is low (2.8). Midbrain has the highest myelin density but the lowest M1 brake — consistent with efficient ongoing remyelination or high mature OL turnover. Cerebellum shows a similar pattern (low Chrm1 = 2.1, high Plp1 = 14.0). This suggests that in regions where remyelination is already proceeding well, M1 tonic inhibition is minimal — perhaps because differentiating OLs downregulate Chrm1 as they mature.

Therapeutic opportunity map: M1 brake vs. OL lineage density

The scatter makes the regional pattern concrete:

  • Isocortex and hippocampus sit in the upper-right quadrant — high OL lineage density, high M1 brake. These are the regions where M1 antagonism would remove the strongest inhibitory signal from the most OPCs. The lab's clinical trials (ReCOVER, ReVIVE) focus on optic neuritis and cortical demyelination — exactly this territory.
  • Midbrain and cerebellum are lower-left — high mature myelin already, low M1 brake. These structures may be efficient remyelinators intrinsically, requiring less pharmacological intervention but also offering less drug effect.
  • Striatum is middle-right — high M1 (13.1) but also very high Plp1 (18.6), suggesting the OL population there is already partially differentiated. The high Chrm4 (12.5) makes striatum a region where M1-selective vs. M4-selective effects can be dissociated.

Why this connects to the lab's work

  • Mechanistic screening (NIH R01NS095889 — GPCR library): The lab is screening GPCR compounds for remyelination activity. Understanding which brain regions have the highest Chrm1 co-expression with OL lineage markers provides a regional readout for where in vivo pharmacological validation would show the clearest signal. I'd like to contribute to the functional validation pipeline — both cell biology experiments characterizing OPC differentiation and computational analysis of expression or imaging data.
  • VEP as a biomarker (NIH R01NS105741): The lab uses visual evoked potentials to measure remyelination in the optic nerve — a region not captured in this atlas analysis but directly analogous to the cortical OPC story (optic nerve OPCs also express M1). I'm interested in contributing to the quantitative analysis of VEP and OCT data as remyelination readouts, particularly in connecting the electrophysiological signal back to the underlying cellular mechanisms.
  • My background spans molecular and cell biology wet lab skills and computational data analysis. I think my combination of practical lab experience and computational background is where I can be most useful to your lab.

Methods

Data retrieved July 2026. Allen Mouse Brain Atlas (Allen Institute for Brain Science, 2004), in situ hybridization, adult mouse (P56), via RMA API at api.brain-map.org/api/v2/data/query.json. Structures: Isocortex (315), Hippocampal formation (1089), Cerebellum (512), Striatum/Caudoputamen (672), Hypothalamus (1097), Midbrain (313), Thalamus (549), Olfactory bulb (507). Corpus callosum (776) excluded — ISH returns zero in fiber tracts (no cell bodies). Expression metric: expression_energy from StructureUnionize (sagittal ISH preferred). Figures: pure Python SVG. Code: fetch_data.py, make_figures.py. Raw data: expression.tsv.
